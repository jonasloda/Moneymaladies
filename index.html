MONEY MALADIES

Within the mainstream economic tradition, money has largely been pushed to the margins of analysis, treated as little more than a veil that obscures the underlying processes of producing and allocating commodities. The methodological objective of the economist, consequently, has often been to lift the money veil in order to “exhibit the real face of the economy” (Robert Liefmann quoted in Patinkin, 1989, p. 140), a move that typically involves focusing instead on the so-called real determinants of economic activity, such as consumer preferences, production technologies, and the initial distribution of resources.

Nowhere is this sidelining of money more conspicuous than in the equilibrium models that dominate economic orthodoxy1. Here, money is assumed—at least in the long run—to influence only nominal variables, such as the general price level and wages, and not real ones, like how many loaves of bread a wage can buy. The underlying assumption, then, is that money is more or less neutral with respect to real economic outcomes.

This, of course, runs counter to most people’s idea of money. Most of us seem to know, at least intuitively, that money is not neutral. It is the tool with which kingdoms and companies are built and sometimes brought down. In this sense, money matters.

So why has something so central been pushed to the margins of economic analysis? Perhaps it is because money, despite being a human artifact, is hard to define, and it is harder still to explain the mechanisms through which it affects the economy. Many writers on the topic therefore find themselves struck by a “kind of intellectual vertigo”, as William Stanley Jevons (1896, p. 217) once wrote, disoriented by the complexity of a phenomenon that appears deceptively simple, making it all the easier to ignore.

Or maybe it is because taking money seriously forces economists to confront questions they would rather avoid, questions about power and politics. Because if money is not just a neutral medium of exchange, it must by defintion be non-neutral, that is, a force that actively shapes economic activities, rather than merely facilitating them. Economists, then, are faced with the uncomfortable realisation that markets cannot be reduced to mechanistic and hermetic systems governed by timeless laws, immune to the messiness of history, institutions, and conflict. Instead, they must grapple with economic systems shaped by institutional arrangements that determine how money is created, who gets access to it, and under what conditions. To accept this is to accept that economics cannot be neatly separated from politics.

This, I believe, is where any serious inquiry into economics must begin, not by abstracting away from money, but by confronting it head on, meaning one must define what it is, understand how it exerts power over the economy, and analyse its institutional and political arrangements.

In what follows, I set out to explore money, addressing it both descriptively and prescriptively. Drawing on the expertise of some of history’s most adept money doctors, I will dissect the workings of monetary systems, diagnose their malfunctions, and propose potential remedies, forming the basis of what may eventually become a money monograph, provisionally titled Money Maladies. Analogous to the alchemist’s pursuit of the philosopher’s stone, my ambition is to extract some kind of theoretical substance that enables us—the visionaries and monetary heretics—to transmute a faltering monetary system, and with it the economy, into something more equitable, however ambiguous that term might be.

Admittedly, this is no easy task and may easily be dismissed as the idealistic quest of someone overly naïve to the complexities of the subject. I hope to dispel such doubts. To persuade the readership and reach a point where valuable insights can be made, I seek to approach the subject with the clarity and rigor it deserves.

As a first and admittedly humble step in this decade-long endeavor (decade-long, as this is strictly an after-hours affair), I have uploaded the opening chapter titled On the Nature of Money. In it, I try to make sense of money. To do so, I bring together two perspectives which, when synthesised, form what I believe to be a coherent account of this enigmatic object, one that reworks and revives ideas articulated by thinkers such as Henry Dunning Macleod and Joseph Schumpeter.

The chapter is structured into five sections:

On the Nature of Money: Prelude
The Money-Relation: A Credit View
The Money-Thing: An Accounting View
Moneyness: Money on a Spectrum
On the Nature of Money: Postlude

I have also started working on a second chapter titled On the Nature of Finance, in which I define finance as a legally constituted creditor–debtor relation that forms the superstructure of money. So far, the chapter consists of three sections:

On The Nature of Finance: Prelude
Lending: Reallocating Money
Banking: Creating Money

As I continue reading, distilling, and transforming thoughts into text, I will steadily add to this body of work.

Naturally, I do not pretend to have all the answers. Recognising the limits of solitary thought, I have therefore included a comment section. Consider it an open invitation to help refine and challenge the ideas presented here2.

Based on the dialogues that emerge, and as my thinking evolves, some sections might change. In that sense, this monograph is not a finished piece, but a living organism. It is open-ended, in progress, and hopefully worth continuing.
